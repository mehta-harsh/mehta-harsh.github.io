Research Interests

I am interested in a variety of topics in the field of Astrophysics and Cosmology. My current research mostly focuses on probing Fundamental Physics using the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) at small scales. The CMB is a nearly uniform relic radiation originating from the Big Bang, following a Planck Black-body spectrum. Not only is the CMB used to study Early-Universe Physics, but its observed properties today are further shaped by interactions between CMB photons and large-scale structure along their path to us. It shows temperature variations at the level of one part in 10⁵ and can be used to test any modifications in the Standard Model of Cosmology and Particle physics.

Axions or ALPs

The CMB can be used to detect hypothetical particles such as axions or axion-like particles (ALPs), one of the dark matter candidates predicted by various Beyond Standard Model (BSM) theories. If ALPs exist in nature, CMB photons can convert into ALPs, leaving imprints through polarization and spectral distortions. I study how these signals arise in galaxy clusters and the Milky Way.

Reionization

Recent JWST observations of massive galaxies at early times of reionization have challenged our undertanding of the astrophysics and/or cosmology. I study the physics of cosmic reionization, which marks the time during which the galaxies came into being. Secondary anisotropies, arising from effects such as gravitational lensing of CMB photons, and scattering by ionized gas via the kinematic Sunyaev Zeldovich (kSZ) effect, provide a powerful probe of this epoch of the Universe. Through these signals, the CMB becomes a sensitive tracer of structure formation, baryonic physics, and the evolution of matter on cosmological scales.

Publications

My list of Publications:

  • Modified Cosmology or Modified Galaxy Astrophysics is Driving the z>6 JWST Results? CMB Experiments can discover the Origin in Near Future (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2025)
    The JWST observations of high-redshift massive galaxies have challenged the current ΛCDM model of cosmology. We show that a joint analysis involving CMB lensing and patchy-kSZ signals will provide smoking-gun signatures on whether it is the modifications in cosmology or our understanding of astrophysics that is driving the excess in bright galaxies observed by JWST.
  • The prospect from the upcoming CMB experiment LiteBIRD to discover axion-like particles using Milky Way (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2025)
    The photon-ALP conversion in the Milky way will generate non-Gaussian anisotropies in the CMB at large scales. We show that LiteBIRD will be able to probe the galactic ALP signal and improve the current bounds on the photon-ALP coupling.
  • Turbulence Induced Non-Gaussian Spectral Distortion in the Microwave Sky from Photon-Axion Conversion in Galaxy Clusters (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2025)
    The presence of turbulence in the magnetic fields and electron densities of galaxy clusters will impact the non-Gaussianity of the ALP signal. We have developed a numerical simulation-based technique that simulates the ALP signal in these environments. A cross-spectrum of temperature and polarization observations will also serve as an independent way to search for ALPs.
  • SpectrAx: Spectral Search of ALPs Using Multi-Band Observations of Clusters from SKA, SO, CMB-S4 & eROSITA (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2025)
    We have developed a multi-band pipeline to infer the astrophysics of galaxy clusters and search for ALPs from EM observations of galaxy clusters in radio, X-rays, optical, and microwave bands. This pipeline will act as a bridge connecting galaxy cluster astrophysics with cosmology.
  • A Diffused Background from Axion-like Particles in the Microwave Sky (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2024)
    The clusters that are unresolved in various frequency bands, will create a diffused ALP background in the microwave sky. Such a signal will result in an increase in the CMB power spectrum at high multipoles, following the spectrum of the ALP signal. We show the detectability of the diffused signal by upcoming CMB detectors.
  • A power spectrum approach to search for Axion-like Particles from resolved galaxy clusters using CMB as a backlight (Mehta & Mukherjee, 2024)
    The existence of ALPs will result in a polarized distortion signal in the CMB along the cluster line of sight. The power spectrum of the ALP distortion signal along the cluster line of sight will vary depending on the profiles. The upcoming CMB experiments will be able to provide bounds on the photon-ALP coupling of more than an order of magnitude better than the currently accepted ones.
